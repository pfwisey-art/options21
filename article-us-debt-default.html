The US Has Never Defaulted — But That Doesn't Mean It's Risk-Free

The conventional view is that US Treasuries are the world's risk-free asset. The US controls its own currency, enjoys unmatched institutional credibility, and faces enormous global demand for its debt. Yet with $36 trillion in obligations and interest costs rising, the structural risks are no longer theoretical.

US national debt: $36.22 trillion. Annual interest cost: ~$1 trillion. Annual tax revenue: ~$5 trillion. Bonds maturing 2025: $750 billion. New 20-year bond rate: 5.1%. Projected debt by 2030 under current trajectory: ~$50–53 trillion.

Why the US Has Not Defaulted

Sovereign Currency Issuer

The US government issues all its debt in US dollars — a currency it controls via the Federal Reserve. Technically, it can always create more dollars to meet obligations. This eliminates the risk of a liquidity-driven default of the kind that devastated countries like Argentina, which borrowed in foreign currencies. The mechanism is imperfect — excessive money creation causes inflation — but it does mean the US can never be forced into default the way a company or foreign-currency borrower can.

Massive Global Demand for Treasuries

US Treasury securities are regarded as the safest financial asset on earth. Foreign governments, central banks, pension funds, and institutions hold Treasuries for liquidity, safety, and reserve purposes. This deep, reliable demand makes it straightforward for the US to roll over maturing debt and issue new debt at relatively low interest rates — even during periods of fiscal stress.

Legal and Institutional Framework

The US Constitution grants Congress explicit authority to tax and borrow, creating institutional mechanisms for debt management that most countries lack. The Federal Reserve can purchase Treasuries in secondary markets (quantitative easing) and acts as a lender of last resort. Even during acute political standoffs, the US has always ultimately prioritised servicing its debt.

"Default is not impossible — especially if politics obstructs action or if markets begin to reject US debt. The most realistic default scenario is political, not economic."

Could the US Default in the Future?

The short answer is yes — though the most likely path to default is political, not economic. Here are the three primary risk scenarios:

1. Political Gridlock (Debt Ceiling Crisis)

If Congress fails to raise the debt ceiling, the Treasury can legally be prohibited from issuing new debt even with payments due. In 2011 and 2023, political standoffs came close to triggering exactly this scenario. The Treasury may be forced to delay payments or prioritise interest over other obligations — a situation widely regarded as a technical default even if interest is ultimately paid.

2. Bond Market Rebellion (Investor Confidence Loss)

If investors lose confidence that the US can manage its debt without triggering high inflation or fiscal collapse, they may demand sharply higher yields — or stop buying Treasuries altogether. This could occur if:

  • Debt-to-GDP spirals with no credible plan to contain it
  • The Fed monetises too much debt → inflation spikes → real yields collapse → Treasury loses investor confidence
  • Major foreign holders (notably China and Japan) begin systematically reducing their Treasury positions

3. Inflation as Soft Default

High and persistent inflation reduces the real value of outstanding debt — creditors are repaid in dollars worth less than what they lent. This is sometimes described as a "soft default" because it represents a real transfer of value from creditors to the government without technically missing a payment.

A Worked Scenario: US Debt Dynamics 2025–2029

Debt spiral scenario — illustrative projection
2025
Starting point: $36.22T debt. New fiscal bill adds $3.8T → Total: $40.02T. Tax revenue: $5T. Interest payments: $1T. New 20yr bond rate: 5.1%.
2026–27
Interest rates rise to 6.5% as inflation persists or Fed tightens policy. New borrowing costs increase significantly on all refinancing activity.
2028
Total debt reaches ~$50T driven by compounding interest, deficits, and mandatory spending (Medicare, defence). Interest costs reach $3.25T/year — that's 65% of annual tax revenue.
2028–29
Bond market loses confidence. Investors demand 7% yields. Foreign central banks reduce Treasury holdings. Credit rating agencies consider downgrade.
2029
Technical default risk: Congress refuses to cut spending or raise taxes. Treasury boxed in — interest alone near total revenue. Debt ceiling not raised in time. US misses or delays a payment. Permanent AAA rating loss. Global credit markets freeze.

This scenario is illustrative rather than a base case — but the mechanics are not fantasy. The critical threshold to watch is when interest payments approach or exceed tax revenue, at which point the US loses the ability to service debt from operational cash flow without further borrowing.

National debt trajectory: $36.2T (2025) → ~$53T (2030 est.) · Interest payments growing as debt compounds at higher rates · Tax revenue relatively flat at ~$5T · Average interest rate on new/refinanced debt rising · Critical risk zone: when annual interest cost approaches or exceeds annual tax revenue — currently projected between 2028–2032 under adverse scenarios.

Key Takeaways
  • The US has not defaulted because it controls its own currency, enjoys unmatched institutional credibility, and faces enormous global demand for Treasuries — not because default is structurally impossible.
  • Default is possible — the most realistic path is political (debt ceiling gridlock) rather than economic. In 2011 and 2023, this came dangerously close to reality.
  • The slow-moving risk is a bond market confidence crisis: if investors begin to price in fiscal unsustainability, yields rise, making the debt more expensive to service — creating a self-reinforcing spiral.
  • Annual interest payments (~$1T in 2025) are already approaching 20% of tax revenue — if rates remain elevated through debt refinancing, this ratio could reach 40–65% by 2028–2030.
  • Soft default via inflation remains a real risk — the Fed cannot raise rates aggressively without triggering a debt servicing crisis, creating a potential policy trap.
  • The investment implication: US Treasuries remain the world's safest liquid asset — but "safest" no longer means "risk-free". Duration risk, inflation risk, and political risk all warrant consideration in fixed income allocation.
